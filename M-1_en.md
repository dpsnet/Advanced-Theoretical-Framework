The definition of spectral dimensions is strictly based on thermonuclear diffusion:
   $$d_s = -2\lim_{t\to0^+}\frac{\ln\text{Tr}(e^{-tD^2})}{\ln t}$$

### 2.1 Definition and construction of real Clifford algebra

Let $(V,Q)$ be a finite-dimensional real vector space and its quadratic form, and the real Clifford algebra is defined as the quotient space:
$$\mathrm{Cl}(V,Q)=T(V)/\langle v\otimes v- Q(v)\cdot 1\rangle$$

where $T(V) = \bigoplus_{k=0}^{\infty} V^{\otimes k}$ is the tensor algebra, ideally generated by the elements $v\otimes v - Q(v)\cdot 1$. Clifford multiplication satisfies the basic relationship:
$$uv+vu=2\langle u,v\rangle, \quad \forall u,v \in V$$

Let $p,q$ be the symbols of $Q$ (the number of positive and negative eigenvalues), denote $\mathrm{Cl}_{p,q} = \mathrm{Cl}(\mathbb{R}^{p,q},Q)$.

The definition and structure of this chapter strictly follow the framework established by Chevalley in his classic book "The Algebraic Theory of Spinors and Clifford Algebras" [1]. Chevalley systematically expounded the algebraic theory of Clifford algebra, laying a solid foundation for the construction of subsequent spinor representations. Compared with Chevalley's classic work, this module further extends Clifford algebra to the context of fractal space-time and studies its representation properties on fractal measure spaces, which is an important expansion of the classical theory.

**Theorem 2.1.1** (general property): For any associative algebra $A$ and linear map $\phi: V \to A$ satisfying $\phi(v)^2 = Q(v) \cdot 1_A$, there exists a unique algebraic homomorphism $\tilde{\phi}: \mathrm{Cl}(V,Q) \to A$ such that $\tilde{\phi} \circ i = \phi$, where $i: V \to \mathrm{Cl}(V,Q)$ is an inclusion mapping.

**Strict proof**:

1. **Existence**:
   - First, consider the tensor algebra $T(V) = \bigoplus_{n=0}^\infty V^{\otimes n}$, where $V^{\otimes 0} = \mathbb{F}$ (base domain), $V^{\otimes 1} = V$, $V^{\otimes n} = V \otimes V \otimes \cdots \otimes V$ ($n$ times tensor product).
   - The Clifford algebra $\mathrm{Cl}(V,Q)$ is defined as the tensor algebra module to the bilateral ideal generated by $\{v \otimes v - Q(v) \cdot 1 \mid v \in V\}$, that is, $\mathrm{Cl}(V,Q) = T(V) / I$, where $I = \langle v \otimes v - Q(v) \cdot 1 \mid v \in V \rangle$.
   - There is a natural homomorphism $\phi_T: T(V) \to A$ in tensor algebra, which is defined as:
     - $\phi_T(1) = 1_A$ (unit unit)
     - $\phi_T(v_1 \otimes v_2 \otimes \cdots \otimes v_n) = \phi(v_1) \phi(v_2) \cdots \phi(v_n)$（对 arbitrary$v_1, \ldots, v_n \in V$）
   - Now verify $\phi_T(I) = \{0\}$:
     - For any $v \in V$, $\phi_T(v \otimes v - Q(v) \cdot 1) = \phi(v)^2 - Q(v) \cdot 1_A = 0$ (by assumption)
     - For any $a \in T(V)$, $b \in I$, $c \in T(V)$, $\phi_T(a b c) = \phi_T(a) \phi_T(b) \phi_T(c) = 0$, because $\phi_T(b) = 0$ ($b \in I$ is a linear combination of generators)
   - Therefore, $\phi_T$ induces an algebraic homomorphism $\tilde{\phi}: \mathrm{Cl}(V,Q) = T(V)/I \to A$, satisfying $\tilde{\phi} \circ \pi = \phi_T$, where $\pi: T(V) \to T(V)/I$ is the quotient mapping.
   - Since $i(v) = \pi(v)$ for any $v \in V$, then $\tilde{\phi} \circ i(v) = \tilde{\phi}(\pi(v)) = \phi_T(v) = \phi(v)$, that is, $\tilde{\phi} \circ i = \phi$.

2. **Uniqueness**:
   - Suppose $\tilde{\phi}_1$ and $\tilde{\phi}_2$ are two algebraic homomorphisms $\mathrm{Cl}(V,Q) \to A$, satisfying $\tilde{\phi}_1 \circ i = \phi$ and $\tilde{\phi}_2 \circ i = \phi$.
   - Consider $\tilde{\phi}_1 - \tilde{\phi}_2: \mathrm{Cl}(V,Q) \to A$, which is a linear map, and for any $v \in V$, $(\tilde{\phi}_1 - \tilde{\phi}_2)(i(v)) = 0$.
   - Since $\mathrm{Cl}(V,Q)$ is generated by $i(V)$ as an algebra (since the tensor algebra is generated by $V$, the quotient map preserves the generators), therefore $\tilde{\phi}_1 - \tilde{\phi}_2$ is zero on the generators and thus on the entire algebra.
   - Therefore, $\tilde{\phi}_1 = \tilde{\phi}_2$, the uniqueness is proved.

**Physical significance**: The universal property ensures the uniqueness of Clifford algebra in quantum field theory. Regardless of the specific representation method (such as matrix representation, differential operator representation, etc.), its algebraic structure remains unchanged. This provides a solid mathematical foundation for the description of spinor fields in quantum gravity.
